### Hey! I'm Miguel Alvarez

I’m a Software Engineer at *Red Hat*, but if you really want to know what drives me, I’m a lifelong student of the **how** and the **why** behind complex systems. My world is built on Low-Level Programming, Systems Architecture, and Reverse Engineering, but those are just the labels for a much deeper obsession.

What I actually do is deconstruct. I have this constant need to peel back the layers of abstraction we take for granted every day. To me, engineering has never been about memorizing syntax or chasing the latest framework. It’s about mastering the underlying concepts.

I believe that once you truly grasp the mental model of how memory breathes, how execution flows, and how the kernel orchestrates the chaos, the specific language or tool you're using just becomes a detail. I don't just want to use the system. I want to understand the soul of the machine.

### Fedora and the Wii
My journey started with a total "trial by fire" approach. When I was 10 or 11, I began experimenting with Fedora Linux without a guide, driven by nothing but a burning curiosity. I spent those early years accidentally wiping partitions and corrupting bootloaders, which forced me to learn how to recover systems from a terminal before I even knew what a "kernel" actually was.

Around that same time, I became obsessed with the LetterBomb exploit on the Nintendo Wii. Witnessing how a malformed pointer or a buffer overflow could grant total control over a closed system completely changed my worldview. What really cautivated me wasn't just the exploit itself, but the realization that understanding a system is the key to liberating it.

It taught me that software is never truly a black box. It is a logical sequence of instructions that can be analyzed, understood, and redirected. My laboratory back then was a modest eMachines laptop gifted by my mother. I spent my nights there writing Visual Basic scripts and learning the basics of memory manipulation, chasing that feeling of breaking through a system's limits just by knowing how it worked under the hood.

### From Game Modding to Systems Engineering
In my early teens, I transitioned into heavy game modding, which became my first real deep dive into Software Engineering. I spent years building automation tools with Tampermonkey, reverse-engineering WebSockets, and dissecting binary protocols to understand how networking actually functioned. Not because I wanted to be a developer, but because I wanted to understand how things worked under the hood.

Because of this, I didn't fit the mold in school. I was distinguished in Mathematics, but beyond that, I didn't really focus much on the formal curriculum. I spent the vast majority of my time at home, teaching myself the inner workings of operating systems, CPU architectures, and the nuances of C and Assembly.

This "bottom-up" learning style gave me a unique perspective. I don't just see a high-level API. I see the syscalls, the context switches, and the cache hits happening behind the scenes. For me, the real education happened at my desk, deconstructing the world one bit at a time.

### You don't have to be like me

I have been a complete engineering obsessive for as long as I can remember. Since I was a child, my mind has operated on a constant, relentless 24/7 cycle. I don't just "do" engineering, I live inside it.

For me, reality is something that needs to be parsed. When I look at a piece of software, I’m not just seeing a UI or a feature, my brain is automatically tracing the execution path, calculating memory offsets, and imagining the syscalls happening in the background. It’s an involuntary process. This obsession means I’m never truly "off." I spend my days deconstructing systems in my head, and my nights are often consumed by the "white noise" of a complex problem.

I’ve spent countless nights staring at a terminal, not because I had a deadline, but because a specific theoretical edge case or a subtle bug wouldn't let me sleep. There is a specific kind of internal pressure that only dissipates once I’ve reached the "core" of the logic. It's the only way I know how to exist. If you’re here looking for a casual perspective, you might find my approach a bit extreme, and that's okay.

### Why I'm writing this blog

I have always been a tinkerer, the kind of person who can’t just use a tool without needing to understand exactly how it works under the hood. For me, the real beauty of technology isn't in the final product, but in the logic that holds it all together.

This blog is a reflection of that obsession, but it is also born out of my own unconventional path. I didn't have a roadmap or a traditional guide to follow, I had to build my own map through trial, error, and countless hours of solitary deconstruction. I’ve always been a bit of a "classic hacker" in that sense, I'm much more comfortable and at home behind a terminal than at a party.

That is why I’m writing this. I want to share my experiments and discoveries to help others navigate these systems that often feel like black boxes. I want to be the resource I wish I had when I was starting out, helping anyone who, like me, believes that the best way to truly learn a system is to take it apart until you can see it breathe.

### My Professional Life
Now at **Red Hat**, my career has come full circle. I’m working within the ecosystem that first sparked my interest in Linux.

My goal is always **to reach the core of the problem**, I really enjoy more than anything the feeling of finally understanding how something works.

Welcome to my personal archives—a collection of notes, experiments, and deep dives into the machines that run our world.

Opinions are my own.
